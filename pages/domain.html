Research Domain

Literature Survey

Academic stress among university students has become a growing concern due to increasing academic workload, tight deadlines, and performance pressure. Existing digital mental health applications such as Headspace, Calm, and Daylio primarily focus on general wellness and meditation practices. While these platforms support emotional well-being, they lack personalization and are not specifically tailored to academic environments.

Recent studies highlight the importance of integrating behavioral data such as sleep and activity with self-reported emotional states to better understand stress patterns. Additionally, machine learning approaches such as clustering and time-series forecasting have been explored for mental health prediction, but their application in academic stress management remains limited.

Furthermore, voice-based emotion detection and natural language processing-driven counseling systems are emerging as promising techniques for improving user engagement and accessibility in mental health support systems.

Research Gap

  • Most systems are reactive, identifying stress only after it occurs.
  • Lack of personalization based on individual behavioral patterns.
  • Minimal integration of multi-modal data such as voice, mood, sleep, and academic workload.
  • Absence of academic-specific stress analysis.
  • No effective use of predictive models for proactive intervention.
  • Limited support for anonymous and stigma-free counseling.

Research Problem

How can a personalized, data-driven system be designed to accurately predict short-term academic stress trends, provide adaptive and meaningful visualizations of stress patterns, enable early intervention before stress peaks occur, and support students through anonymous and accessible mental health resources?

Research Objectives

Main Objective: To develop an intelligent digital companion that helps university students monitor, predict, and manage academic stress proactively.

Specific Objectives:

  • To collect and analyze multi-modal user data such as mood, sleep, voice, and academic context.
  • To identify individual stress patterns through stress fingerprinting.
  • To develop a hybrid machine learning model for stress prediction.
  • To design an adaptive heatmap visualization for stress representation.
  • To implement real-time coping mechanisms and interventions.
  • To provide anonymous counseling support using NLP techniques.

Methodology

  1. Data Collection: Active inputs include mood logs, voice journals, and academic deadlines, while passive inputs include sleep patterns and activity data.
  2. Stress Fingerprinting: Extract features from behavioral data and apply clustering techniques such as K-Means and DBSCAN to classify stress types like deadline-sensitive and sleep-reactive.
  3. Stress Forecasting: Use time-series and machine learning models, including Random Forest and LSTM, to predict stress levels 3 to 5 days ahead.
  4. Visualization: Present past, present, and predicted stress levels using an adaptive calendar heatmap.
  5. Intervention and Support: Deliver personalized recommendations, adaptive audio-based relaxation soundscapes, anonymous NLP-based counseling, and community-based peer support via voice analysis.
